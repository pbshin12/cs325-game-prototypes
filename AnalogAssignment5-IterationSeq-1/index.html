  • Technical Merit: What is interesting about your game technology? / Why I do not like Phaser

    This game is made using the Unity game engine. There are several reasons why I chose not to use Phaser 3. Before I begin, I want to mention that I am spoiled by the convenience of Unity so I am definitely biased against Phaser 3. First, it is extremely difficult to see where I am placing my sprites and game objects. Having to figure out where to place objects through trial and error wastes a lot of my time whereas in Unity I can simply click and drag and know exactly where it is placed. It was also convenient to click and drag audio files onto the Unity interface and have it ready. Second, I was able to easily create groups without having to manually guess and check where to place objects within said group. This is especially apparent when it comes to hitboxes. Thirdly, I felt creatively restricted when using Phaser and I often do not know what I'm looking for because I can't find what I needed. What's frustrating is that many of the examples are outdated and I often spend hours searching for something end up empty-handed. In Unity, there are thousands of Youtube videos on nearly everything and I was able to figure out what I needed fairly quickly. Also, Unity's documentation is much easier to read and follow than Phaser's, they provide examples as to how it should look like. Fourth, I had a much easier time managing colliders and triggers in Unity since Unity has built-in functions that handle that. Phaser does have collision detection, but it is often very confusing and I often lose track of the many objects that interact with each other. Whereas in Unity, I can generalize a specific group of objects by either referring to their tag, or even their name, which is very convenient. Lastly, I was a bit frustrated with the Update() function in Phaser 3, especially when it comes to basic controls. There was a consensus amongst several of my classmates where they had encountered a strange bug where their character will be stuck moving in one direction. There were also complaints of strange interactions with different objects whenever they try to implement physics. The fix in Unity would be to use FixedUpdate(), but I had an extremely difficult time finding the equivalent in Phaser. The same can be said regarding DeltaTime, which enables me to record the relative time for a given event.

  • Gameplay: A paragraph describing the actions the player can perform, the system dynamics, and the core mechanic. Include a concise explanation of the prototypeâ€™s inputs and their expected effects (how to play). You can also describe game play that is not in the prototype. You may include mock-up images for parts of the game not in the prototype.

    Players can only move vertically. Like Flappy Bird, the movement involves "hops" by pressing either the W or SPACE key and gravity will gradually pull them down. Touching the bottom will instantly kill the player, so be careful. The objective of the game is to defeat the Necromancer and not die. To achieve this, the player can shoot fireballs at the boss by pressing J. The boss has 3 different phases, which will add a new challenge the longer the game progresses.

  • Strategies: What player strategies do you expect will be effective at playing this game?

    The gravity is actually a bit weak, which makes the character "hover" a slight bit before coming back down. Being able to time each hop enables the player to easily dodge obstacles and projectiles.
